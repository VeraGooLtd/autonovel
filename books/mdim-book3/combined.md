# The Sentinel Ridge Showdown

By Danny Ryan


# Chapter 1: The Morning After

If there is one thing you need to know about telepathic headaches, it's that they are NOT like normal headaches.

Normal headaches are like someone gently tapping the inside of your skull with a tiny hammer. Telepathic headaches are like someone has replaced the tiny hammer with a full-sized construction drill and forgotten to turn it off.

I lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, trying to remember if I'd eaten enough protein yesterday. Barnaby was very clear about protein. "The human brain is a fragile instrument, Beatrice," he'd said at least forty-seven times. "It requires fuel. Specifically, the smoked turkey I saw in the bin."

The bin. Right. The bin that Mummy had already put out for the collectors.

I rolled over and checked my notebook. It was open to yesterday's page, where I'd drawn a map of Riverstone with all the Meow-fia sightings marked in red. There were a lot of red marks. Too many for coincidence.

My head throbbed again.

*Good morning, Beatrice,* Barnaby's voice echoed in my skull. It sounded like a very old teacher who had spent too much time in a dusty library and not enough time in the sun. *I trust you slept well. I didn't. Someone has been sleeping on my rug.*

I sat up too fast. The room spun.

"Barnaby," I whispered. "What do you mean, someone's been sleeping on your rug?"

*I mean exactly what I said. There is fur on my rug. It is not my fur. It is long and ginger and it smells like...* He paused. *...expensive cat food and criminal intent.*

Ginger. That could only mean one cat.

"Simba," I said.

*Presumably. Though I refuse to give him the satisfaction of a name. He is "the intruder" until further notice.*

I grabbed my notebook and started a new page. EVIDENCE OF SIMBA'S RETURN:
- Ginger fur on Barnaby's rug
- My telepathic headache (side effect of pinging)
- The feeling that something terrible is about to happen

That last one wasn't exactly scientific. But in my experience, it was usually right.

**[Doodle: A bulldog lying on a rug with ginger fur on it, looking deeply offended. Caption: "INTRUDER ALERT"]**

"Beatrice! Five minutes!" Mummy's voice rang from the hallway. I could hear the *clack-clack-clack* of her shoes. That was her "Busy Monday" walk. It meant the schedule was already behind and she was going to speed up.

I looked at the schedule on my wall. Today was Neon Violet.

Neon Violet was the worst color. It meant "everything is happening at once and nothing will go according to plan."

I got dressed, grabbed my detective notebook, and headed downstairs. Barnaby was already at his bowl, staring at his food with deep suspicion.

"It's the same food as always, Barnaby," I said.

*It's not the food I'm suspicious of. It's the day. Mondays are statistically the most likely day for catastrophic events.*

"That's not true."

*I've been keeping data. Would you like to see my charts?*

"No."

*I'll show you anyway. I've drawn them on a napkin.*

He had, in fact, drawn charts on a napkin. With his paw. In what appeared to be ketchup.

**[Doodle: A ketchup-stained napkin with a bar chart showing "Catastrophic Events by Day of Week" — Monday is the tallest bar]**

Mummy appeared in the doorway, her hair in a bun so tight it pulled her eyebrows into a permanent expression of mild surprise. "Bea, you're late. And why is there ketchup on the table?"

"Barnaby's doing statistical analysis."

"Dogs don't do statistics."

"This one does. He's very judgmental about it."

*Thank you. I try.*

Mummy sighed. It was her "I love my daughter but she's weird" sigh. I'd catalogued it as Sigh #47 in my notebook.

"Neon Violet day," she said, checking her clipboard. "School, then Maya's for the Science Fair project, then Lulu has swimming. Don't forget your detective notebook — you have that meeting with Mr. Hendricks about the school newspaper."

I did NOT want to think about the school newspaper. Mr. Hendricks wanted me to write "appropriate" stories. I wanted to write about the Meow-fia. We had reached an impasse.

"Also," Mummy added, "Mrs. Patel called. She wants us to come by the café after school. Something about a 'situation.'"

My head throbbed again. That construction drill feeling.

"What kind of situation?" I asked.

*She didn't say. But I detected a note of panic in her voice. And the faint scent of feline hubris.*

Feline hubris. That was Barnaby's way of saying "Simba is involved."

This was going to be a very long day.

# Chapter 2: The Accusation

By the time we reached school, Barnaby had accused three pigeons, a traffic cone, and my left shoe of being in league with Simba.

This was unusual, even for him.

Usually Barnaby saved his accusations for people who deserved them, like the postman, the neighbour with the leaf blower, and any human who said, "Who's a good boy?" in that high squeaky voice. But today he was staring out of the car window with his eyes narrowed, which made his jowls wobble in a very suspicious way.

*'Feline hubris,'* he said.

I looked up from my notebook. "Can you say that without sounding like you swallowed a dictionary?"

*'Cats are being smug on purpose.'*

"That's better. Also ruder."

*'Thank you.'*

Riverstone looked normal from the car. Too normal. The baker was putting trays of sausage rolls in the window. Mr. Hendricks was unlocking the school gates. Two Year 2s were arguing about whether a conker could survive being put in a sandwich.

But normal was exactly what Simba liked to hide behind.

Mummy parked in the drop-off lane and immediately checked her clipboard. "Bea, your reading folder, your PE kit, your recorder, and your permission slip for the museum trip."

"I don't have a permission slip."

"Exactly."

"That's not exactly. That's the opposite of exactly."

Mummy gave me the look that meant I was being logical at the wrong time. "It's in your bag."

I checked. It was in my bag. Mummy was annoyingly good at being right.

Barnaby sniffed the air as I climbed out. His ears pricked.

*'There,'* he said. *'Do you smell it?'*

I smelled toast, wet coats, and the school dinners van. "No."

*'Of course you don't. Humans smell like boiled potatoes with shoes.'*

"Thanks."

*'You're welcome.'*

I followed his gaze to the playground wall. Someone had stuck a poster there. It was printed on bright orange paper, which is the colour people use when they want you to panic but also read their handwriting.

MISSING: DIAMOND COLLAR

Below that was a picture of a very fluffy Persian cat wearing a collar that looked more expensive than Daddy's car. The cat had the sort of face that suggested it knew the price of everything and approved of none of it.

Barnaby made a low growling sound.

*'Mrs. Whiskers,'* he said. *'Persian. Dramatic. Likely impossible to travel with unless carried in a handbag shaped like a throne.'*

"How do you know her?"

*'She sits on the wall outside the Crumb & Kettle and judges the pigeons. Professionally.'*

Before I could ask what professional pigeon judging involved, Mrs. Whiskers herself appeared. She was being carried by Mrs. Patel from the café next door, wrapped in a pink blanket like a royal baby. Mrs. Patel looked pale.

"Beatrice!" she called. "Can you help me, please?"

Mummy turned. "Mrs. Patel, is everything all right?"

"No, no, I think so. My cat collar has gone. Mrs. Whiskers says Barnaby took it."

Barnaby's head snapped round so fast his ears flapped.

*'I beg your pardon?'*

I put a hand on his head before he could launch a legal complaint. "Barnaby took it?"

Mrs. Patel winced. "Mrs. Whiskers is very certain."

Mrs. Whiskers stared at Barnaby. Barnaby stared back.

It was like watching two lawyers meet in a lift.

*'Madam,'* Barnaby said, loud enough in my head to make me wince, *'I have never stolen jewellery in my life. I have standards.'*

Mrs. Whiskers blinked slowly.

Then she sneezed.

Mrs. Patel patted her. "She says he did."

"How do you know what she says?"

Mrs. Patel went even paler. "I... cat language?"

Mummy smiled politely. Mummy's polite smile is what she uses when someone says something strange but she doesn't want to be rude. "Well, I'm sure it's a misunderstanding."

But Mrs. Whiskers lifted one paw and pointed.

At Barnaby.

Then she pointed again.

At Barnaby's collar.

I looked.

There, caught in the fur under Barnaby's blue collar, was one tiny silver thread.

My stomach did a small flip.

Barnaby noticed me noticing.

*'Do not look at me like that, Beatrice.'*

"I'm not."

*'You are. Your face has gone all detective-shaped.'*

Mrs. Patel leaned closer. "Oh dear. Is that...?"

I pinched the thread carefully and pulled it free. It was silver, shiny, and definitely not from Barnaby's collar.

It looked exactly like the sort of thread you'd find on a very expensive cat blanket.

Barnaby went very still.

*'This,'* he said, *'is a setup.'*

I wanted to believe him. I did. Barnaby was arrogant, bossy, and once tried to train me to open tins by barking in Morse code, but he wasn't a thief.

Except the evidence was now sitting in my hand.

Mrs. Whiskers gave a tiny, smug meow.

Barnaby narrowed his eyes.

*'Oh, this is war.'*

**[Doodle: Barnaby in a police lineup with three cats, all wearing tiny sunglasses. Barnaby has a sign saying: "I HAVE STANDARDS." Caption: "THE SUSPECT"]**

Mummy made me apologise to Mrs. Patel, which felt unfair because Barnaby hadn't done anything. Barnaby made me write down the evidence instead, which felt better.

Evidence:
1. Mrs. Whiskers' diamond collar missing.
2. Silver thread found on Barnaby's collar.
3. Mrs. Whiskers accusing Barnaby.
4. Mrs. Whiskers looking far too pleased with herself.
5. Barnaby saying "this is war" in my head, which is not evidence but probably relevant.

At lunch, Maya found me under the climbing frame with my notebook.

"You look worried," she said.

"I'm not worried. I'm investigating."

"That's what worried people call it."

Maya sat beside me and opened her lunchbox. She had cut her carrots into tiny rectangles and labelled the sections "crunch data". Maya treats lunch like a science experiment, which is why I like her.

I showed her the thread.

Maya held it up to the light. "Metallic fibre. Could be from a collar, but not necessarily."

"Mrs. Whiskers says Barnaby stole it."

"Mrs. Whiskers is a cat."

"Exactly."

Maya nodded as if this explained everything. "Cats are basically tiny liquid lawyers."

Barnaby, who was pretending to sleep beside my bag, opened one eye.

*'I heard that, Sharma.'*

Maya froze. Then she sighed. "Still weird. Still useful."

She pulled a small device from her pocket. It looked like a remote control that had been built by a toaster.

"What's that?" I asked.

"Prototype Mark Two Meow-fia Signal Detector."

"From Book— I mean, from your Science Fair project?"

Maya glanced around. "Yes. From my Science Fair project. Definitely not from the thing we are not allowed to call Book 2."

Barnaby sat up. *'Does it detect criminal intent?'*

"No."

*'Useless.'*

"It detects radio bursts in the 433 megahertz range. Which is the range Simba used last time."

I looked at her properly. "You built it?"

"Mostly. Daddy helped solder the bit I wasn't allowed to touch."

"That sounds like most of Daddy's projects."

Maya switched it on. A tiny red light blinked.

"See? Nothing."

Then the red light turned green.

Then it beeped.

Once.

Twice.

Then it started beeping so fast it sounded like an angry microwave.

Barnaby's ears flattened.

*'Well,'* he said. *'That's inconvenient.'*

Across the playground, near the bins, a ginger tail vanished behind a dustbin.

# Chapter 3: The Meow-fia Strikes

After school, Riverstone stopped being a town and became a crime scene with bunting.

The first call came from Mr. Pritchard at the newsagent. His window display of chocolate mice had been raided. Not stolen. Raided. Every mouse had been arranged in a perfect circle around a single note.

The note said: ANIMALS DESERVE SNACK JUSTICE.

The second call came from the school kitchen. Someone had opened the fridge and removed forty-seven cheese triangles.

The third came from the pet shop, where the hamster pellets were gone, the gerbil treats were gone, and Mr. Singh's prize parrot had been found wearing a tiny paper crown and looking personally offended.

By four o'clock, my notebook had six pages of thefts.

Maya stood behind me at the park bench, watching the red light on her detector blink like it had opinions.

"Same signal," she said.

"Same signal as what?"

"The Book 2 thing."

"We are not allowed to call it that."

"Fine. The previous incident."

Barnaby paced in front of us, nose to the ground. "The pattern is obvious. Simba is escalating."

I wrote it down.

PATTERN:
- Multiple thefts
- All animal food
- All in public places
- Notes left behind
- No human witnesses
- Barnaby blamed for at least one

Maya frowned at the detector. "There's something else. The signal isn't coming from one place. It's bouncing."

"Bouncing?"

"Like a message being sent from lots of points. Or like someone wants us to think that."

Barnaby sat down heavily. *'Cats. Always making everything theatrical.'*

A shadow fell over the bench.

"Beatrice Chen?"

I looked up. It was Mrs. Whiskers.

Well, not Mrs. Whiskers exactly. Mrs. Patel was holding her again. The cat wore her blank diamond collar, which was strange because I thought it had gone missing.

Mrs. Patel looked embarrassed. "Sorry. Mrs. Whiskers found it. In the café cupboard."

Barnaby's eyes narrowed.

*'How convenient.'*

Mrs. Whiskers looked at him and licked one paw.

I looked at the collar. It was shiny. Too shiny. The silver thread was gone.

"Mrs. Patel," I said carefully, "did Mrs. Whiskers find it herself?"

Mrs. Patel blinked. "She... meowed at the cupboard until I opened it."

Barnaby made a noise that was not quite a growl and not quite a laugh.

*'Riveting testimony. The cupboard confessed.'*

Mrs. Patel hurried away.

Maya watched Mrs. Patel hurry away, shoulders tight.

"She looked like when Mummy finds out Daddy has bought another drill."

"That scared?"

"Worse. Café-owner scared."

Barnaby sniffed the air where Mrs. Patel had been standing. *'She smells of cinnamon, flour, fear, and... tuna pâté.'"*

"That last one is probably you."

*'It is not.'*

Maya's detector beeped again. This time the green light pulsed slowly, like a heartbeat.

"There," she said, pointing toward the alley behind the Crumb & Kettle.

We followed.

The alley was narrow and smelled of bin bags and yesterday's chips. At the back, behind the café, someone had left a saucer of cream. Beside it was a scrap of paper with a tiny paw print drawn in blue-grey ink.

I knew that paw print.

Simba.

Barnaby lowered his voice. *'The Ginger Ninja marks territory like a Victorian villain with stationery.'*

I picked up the paper with my pencil. On the back, written in tiny careful letters, were three words:

SENTINEL RIDGE.

Maya's face changed. "That's where the old transmitter is."

"And where the Festival fireworks go off," I said.

Barnaby looked from the note to the sky.

*'Oh,'* he said. *'He's not stealing snacks.'*

The detector beeped faster.

*'He's stealing everything.'*

# Chapter 4: The Human Contact

The Crumb & Kettle was warm enough to fog my glasses.

That usually made me happy. Warm cafés smell like butter, sugar, and people making better choices than I am. But today the café felt wrong. Mrs. Patel kept glancing at the door. Every time the bell rang, she jumped.

Barnaby sat under our table and pretended not to stare at the sausage rolls.

*'I am merely observing pastry security,'* he said.

"You're drooling."

*'Strategic moisture.'*

Maya had her detector hidden inside her backpack. It was wrapped in a sock, which she said reduced interference. I said it made it look like a haunted foot.

Mrs. Patel brought us hot chocolate and three cinnamon buns.

"On the house," she said.

Barnaby's head came up. *'At last. A human with sense.'*

Mrs. Patel noticed him looking. "You can have a plain biscuit, Barnaby."

Barnaby looked wounded. *'Plain? Beatrice, write this down. I have been insulted in my own café.'*

I stirred my hot chocolate. "Mrs. Patel, why were you feeding strays behind the café?"

Her spoon stopped halfway to the saucer.

Maya looked at me. I looked at Barnaby. Barnaby looked at the sausage rolls, because Barnaby has no subtlety when pastry is involved.

Mrs. Patel gave a small laugh. "Oh, you saw that?"

"We saw the cream."

"And the note."

Her face went still.

The café bell rang. Mrs. Patel turned so fast her apron strings flapped.

A ginger cat slipped in through the door.

Not Simba. Smaller. Scruffy. One ear bent like it had lost an argument with a envelope. It walked to Mrs. Patel and meowed.

Mrs. Patel picked it up and hugged it against her chest.

"Hello, Pickles," she whispered.

Barnaby's ears pricked. *'Blackmail cat.'*

"How do you know?" I whispered back.

*'Because no cat that fluffy chooses that name.'*

Mrs. Patel carried Pickles through the swinging door into the kitchen. I waited three seconds, then stood.

"Bea," Maya hissed. "What are you doing?"

"Finding out why a café owner looks like she's about to cry into the muffins."

I pushed through the kitchen door.

Mrs. Patel was at the sink, holding Pickles with one hand and wiping her eyes with her sleeve. On the counter was a photo.

A cat in a tiny cage.

Not Pickles. A different cat. Grey, with one white paw.

Mrs. Patel shoved the photo under a tea towel when she saw me.

"I'm sorry, Beatrice. I didn't mean—"

"Is that your cat?"

She shook her head. "My sister's. Marmalade. She lives above the bakery."

"Someone took her?"

Mrs. Patel looked at the door. "They said if I didn't help, Marmalade would stay in the dark."

Barnaby's voice came into my head, low and sharp. *'Simba.'*

Maya appeared behind me with the detector. The red light was glowing steady now.

"Mrs. Patel," Maya said gently, "did Simba make you help him?"

Mrs. Patel sat down on a flour sack. "He said animals were tired of being told when to eat, where to sleep, who to obey. He said he would free them."

"That sounds nice when cats say it," I said. "It sounds less nice when they steal everyone's food."

"He has my sister's cat."

Barnaby stepped forward. *'Then we get the cat back.'*

Mrs. Patel stared at him.

I stared at her.

Maya stared at all of us.

Mrs. Patel whispered, "You can hear him?"

There are moments when the whole world tilts. This was one.

I had spent years pretending Barnaby was only talking to me because explaining telepathic bulldogs to adults is difficult. Adults hear "my dog talks to me" and start using phrases like "imagination" and "screen time".

But Mrs. Patel was looking at Barnaby as if he had just ordered a cappuccino.

Barnaby lifted his chin. *'Yes. And I have excellent taste in cafés.'*

Mrs. Patel covered her mouth.

Maya leaned close to me. "This is either a breakthrough or a disaster."

"Both," I said.

Mrs. Patel took a shaky breath. "He's using the old transmitter on Sentinel Ridge. Tonight. He has people helping him. Not many. But enough to open the storage cupboards at the festival."

"Who?"

Mrs. Patel looked at the door. "I don't know names. Only voices. One of them keeps saying the blackout must happen before the fireworks."

Maya's detector beeped once.

Barnaby looked up.

*'Then we have less time than I hoped.'*

# Chapter 5: The Ping

I should not have pinged the pigeons.

This is important, because if Mummy ever finds this page, I want it clearly stated that I knew it was a bad idea before I did it.

Bad ideas are still ideas. And I am a detective. Detectives do bad ideas for evidence.

We were behind the Crumb & Kettle, where Mrs. Patel had given us a paper bag of cheese scones "for courage". Barnaby had eaten two and said they were "acceptable, if emotionally shallow". Maya was adjusting her detector. Pickles the blackmail cat was hiding in a cardboard box under the table, looking like a fluffy accusation.

"We need to know how many cats are involved," I said.

Maya did not look up. "The detector can track the signal, not identify cats."

"I wasn't talking to you."

Barnaby froze mid-scone.

*'No.'*

"We need the Aviary Network."

*'Absolutely not.'*

"Feathered Frank owes you."

*'Feathered Frank owes me one worm from 2023. That does not make him my employee.'*

"He also said you saved his tail."

*'He exaggerated. It was mostly my left flank.'*

I closed my eyes and reached for the ping.

Book 2 had taught me that animal thoughts are not like human thoughts. Barnaby is all words, sarcasm, and food rankings. Pigeons are more like bright, fluttery postcards.

I pictured Feathered Frank.

Grey feathers. Nervous eyes. A tiny red scarf he wore because he said it made him look "operationally important".

The ping went out.

For one second, nothing.

Then my head filled with wings.

*'Frank here! Not hiding! Merely stationed! Is this Bea? Why is my brain full of scone?'*

"Barnaby needs information."

*'Barnaby owes me a worm.'*

*'The worm was compensation for your cowardice,'* Barnaby said.

*'Cowardice is a strong word!'*

"Frank," I said, because if I let them argue, we would still be there at Christmas. "Simba is planning something at Sentinel Ridge. How many animals are with him?"

The pigeon thoughts fluttered together. There were lots of them. Pigeons do not think one at a time. They think in a flock, which is like having a group chat inside your skull, except everyone is shouting about crumbs.

*'Many cats,'* Frank said. *'Not all Meow-fia. Some confused. Some hungry. Some following because Simba has speeches.'*

"What about humans?"

The flock went quiet.

*'Two-leg shadows,'* Frank said. *'At the ridge. Opening doors. Moving boxes.'*

Maya went pale. "Human collaborators."

Barnaby's fur rose along his back.

*'That changes the flavour of this crime.'*

"What flavour?"

*'Treasonous pastry.'*

"Frank, when?"

*'Festival night. When the lights go out. The big eating begins.'*

"Where are the food reserves?"

The pigeon thoughts exploded into images: school cupboards, café storerooms, the bakery, the deli counter at Glass Vaults, the community hall, the scout hut, the church hall, the back of Mr. Pritchard's shop.

All of Riverstone's food.

Not snacks this time.

Supplies.

My hand tightened around my pencil.

"Simba isn't doing a heist," I said. "He's doing a blackout and a raid."

Maya's detector gave a sharp, angry beep.

*'Signal source moving,'* she said. "No. Wait. Multiple sources. He's testing transmitters."

Barnaby stared toward the hills.

*'Sentinel Ridge.'*

The ping faded. My head throbbed. Not the construction drill headache from that morning. This was worse. Like someone had put my brain in a washing machine with a spoon.

I sat down quickly.

Barnaby nudged my knee.

*'Beatrice?'*

"I'm fine."

*'You are lying with the subtlety of a falling piano.'*

Maya checked the detector. "We have forty-eight hours."

"Until what?"

She turned the screen toward me.

A countdown blinked in red.

47:59:12

47:59:11

47:59:10

Barnaby swallowed the last bite of scone.

*'Then I suggest we recruit an army.'*

**[Doodle: Bea's head with radio waves coming out, pigeons carrying tiny message scrolls, and Barnaby looking alarmed. Caption: "THE PING"]**

# Chapter 6: The Warning

Adults are very good at not believing children.

They say things like:
- "Are you sure?"
- "That sounds unlikely."
- "Perhaps you've misunderstood."
- "Let's not jump to conclusions."
- "Have you tried not imagining disasters?"

Mummy said all five before I had finished explaining.

We were in the kitchen. Daddy was underneath the sink with a torch in his mouth, which meant he could not interrupt, which was probably for the best. Lulu was drawing on the table with a marker pen that I was fairly sure was permanent.

"So," Mummy said, "let me check I understand. Simba the cat is planning to steal every snack in Riverstone during the Festival blackout using a transmitter on Sentinel Ridge, with help from humans you cannot identify, while Mrs. Patel's sister's cat is being held hostage?"

"Yes."

"And Barnaby told you this?"

"Yes."

Mummy looked at Barnaby.

Barnaby looked back with great dignity.

*'I would like that noted accurately,'* he said.

"He says he would like that noted accurately."

Mummy rubbed her forehead. "Bea, I know you and Barnaby have a special bond."

That was Mummy-code for "my daughter talks to her dog".

"But this is a very big story."

"It's not a story. It's evidence."

I put my notebook on the table.

Mummy looked at the map. The theft locations. The countdown. The words SENTINEL RIDGE.

Her face softened.

"Oh, sweetheart."

I hated that voice. It was the voice she used when I brought home a science project that had exploded in a way the teacher did not appreciate.

"Barnaby is not lying."

"I'm not saying he is."

"You are saying it with your eyebrows."

Daddy's legs emerged from under the sink. "Are we talking about the cat mafia again?"

"Meow-fia," I said.

"Right. Sorry. Important distinction."

Maya arrived with her detector wrapped in a tea towel this time. "Mrs. Chen, the radio signal is real. I can show you the readings."

Mummy took the detector carefully. "Maya, this is very clever. But clever machines can pick up all sorts of things."

"It is not picking up all sorts of things. It is picking up one thing repeatedly from the same direction."

"Could it be a toy?"

Maya looked offended. "It is not a toy. It is a prototype."

Daddy nodded solemnly. "Prototypes are serious. I once prototyped a self-stirring soup spoon."

"What happened?" Mummy asked.

"It stirred the soup. And the table. And my eyebrow."

Mummy sighed. "Not now, David."

Barnaby stood on his back legs and put his paws on the table.

*'Listen to me, woman of schedules.'*

"Barnaby!"

*'No, Beatrice. This is important.'*

Mummy stared. "Did he just call me woman of schedules?"

"Yes."

"That's... actually quite accurate."

Barnaby lowered his voice. *'Your daughter is right. The cat is vain, theatrical, and has terrible ideas about civic organisation. If he succeeds, Riverstone will be without food, the humans will panic, and I will be blamed because of that ridiculous silver thread.'*

Mummy blinked.

Daddy slowly removed the torch from his mouth.

Lulu pointed at Barnaby. "Dog talking."

"Yes, Lulu," Daddy said faintly. "Dog talking."

Mummy sat down.

For a moment, nobody spoke.

Then she said, "I need to phone Mrs. Patel."

Maya smiled. "So you believe us?"

"I believe Mrs. Patel is frightened. And I believe my daughter has found something serious."

Barnaby sat back on his haunches. *'At last. Reason returns to the household.'*

Mummy picked up her phone. "And after that, I believe we are going to need help."

Barnaby's ears perked.

*'Define help.'*

"Police," Mummy said.

Barnaby made a disgusted noise.

*'Humans with hats.'*

# Chapter 7: The Alliance

By Saturday morning, the Canine Resistance had gathered behind the old sports pavilion.

This was less impressive than it sounds.

The Canine Resistance consisted of:
- Barnaby, who considered himself commander
- Henry the Horse, who was not a dog but had been invited because he was brave
- Three spaniels called Biscuit, Pip, and Another Pip
- A terrier named Colonel Socks, who wore one actual sock
- Feathered Frank, who was not a dog but said the pavilion had "better sightlines"
- Me, because nobody else could hear Barnaby properly
- Maya, because she had the detector
- Lulu, because nobody could stop her

Lulu arrived carrying a toy shovel and a jam sandwich.

"I am here for the mission," she announced.

Barnaby looked at the sandwich. "Is that strawberry?"

"Yes."

"Then you may stand near the front."

Maya set up the detector on an upturned bucket. "The signal is stronger. Sentinel Ridge is definitely active."

Henry the Horse lowered his huge head. "I can carry supplies."

"Supplies?" I asked.

He nudged a carrier bag with his nose. Inside were apples, two torches, a blanket, and a traffic cone.

"Why the traffic cone?"

"Safety."

Barnaby approved. "The horse has a point."

Feathered Frank fluttered onto Henry's back. "Pigeon scouts report cats near the ridge. Also a fox, but he says he is neutral."

"Can we trust a neutral fox?" I asked.

From town, it is a green lump with a radio mast on top. From the bottom, it is a giant hill wearing trees and trying to be difficult.

Lulu gasped. "A tree house."

Once.

Twice.

Like an eye opening.

# Chapter 14: The Regroup

The base of Sentinel Ridge was muddy, cold, and full of animals pretending not to be frightened.

# Chapter 16: The Festival

# Chapter 17: The Blackout

The blackout happened during the fireworks.

One moment, Riverstone was full of light. Gold sparks burst over the park. Everyone cheered. Lulu shouted, "AGAIN!" so loudly I heard her from the ridge path.

The next moment, everything went black.

Not dark like bedtime.

Dark like the town had disappeared.

Then people started shouting.

Barnaby's voice came sharp in my head. "Keep low."

We climbed.

The ridge path was steep and slippery. Branches scratched my coat. My torch beam jumped over roots and stones. Behind us, the festival became a mess of voices and phone lights.

Above us, green eyes appeared in the trees.

Cats.

Lots of cats.

They watched us from branches, rocks, and the path ahead.

Barnaby growled. "Do not stop."

"I wasn't planning to."

A cat dropped in front of us. Then another. Then Duke stepped out from the shadows.

I stopped.

Barnaby stopped too.

"Duke," he said.

"Barnaby."

The cats behind Duke shifted.

I could see the transmitter mast above them now. A metal tower with a blinking red box at the top.

Simba sat on the platform beneath it like a tiny general on a very unstable throne.

"Beatrice Chen," he said. "Barnaby. I wondered when the audience would arrive."

Barnaby's lip curled. "If this is another speech, I shall bite the scenery."

Simba ignored him. "Look below. Riverstone is blind. My operatives are moving. By midnight, every cupboard will be open."

Maya's jammer had to be working. The signal was not smooth. It crackled in my skull like bad radio.

But the cats were still listening.

Simba lifted his chin. "You cannot stop liberation."

Barnaby stepped forward. "You built a cult and called it lunch."

Simba's eyes flashed. "Seize them."

The cats moved.

Then the ridge erupted behind us.

Henry came charging up the path with apples flying from his halter. The spaniels barked. Feathered Frank screamed, "SIMBA IS A FRAUD!" from above.

Duke turned.

For one second, everything froze.

Then Duke stepped aside.

"Run," he said.

Barnaby and I ran.

**[Doodle: Riverstone in darkness with glowing cat eyes in the trees and a red transmitter blinking above. Caption: "THE BLACKOUT"]**

# Chapter 18: The Transmitter

The transmitter platform smelled of rain, metal, and Simba's ego.

That is a lot of smell for one small cat.

Barnaby and I reached the mast while Duke held the lower path. Behind him, Henry and the dogs kept the Meow-fia busy. Above us, Feathered Frank flew in circles shouting updates that were mostly panic.

Simba stood beside the red box.

"Do you know what this is?" he asked.

"A crime machine," I said.

"A liberation engine."

"A crime machine."

Barnaby nodded. "The girl is correct."

Simba's tail lashed. "Humans decide everything. When we eat. Where we sleep. Whether we may chase birds, which I do not, because I am disciplined."

"You chased a drone last summer," Barnaby said.

"It was spying."

"It was a child's toy."

"It had a camera."

I stepped toward the red box. Simba moved in front of it.

"You cannot stop it," he said. "The signal is already out."

"Maya is jamming it."

"Maya Sharma is a child with wires."

"Children with wires are dangerous," Barnaby said. "I have seen the evidence."

Simba's voice rose. "Tonight, animals take back Riverstone. No more leads. No more bowls. No more humans saying 'no' because it is Tuesday."

Barnaby's ears flattened. "Some humans say no because you tried to steal a wedding ham."

"It was poorly guarded."

I reached into my coat for Maya's silver scrambler.

Simba saw it.

His eyes widened.

Cats should not be allowed to look that pleased.

"Ah," he said. "The little girl brought a toy."

I held the scrambler behind my back. "Yes. And you brought a speech."

Barnaby glanced at me.

That was my cue.

I threw the scrambler.

Not at Simba.

At Barnaby.

He caught it in his mouth.

Simba lunged for him.

Barnaby ducked.

I grabbed the red box and yanked open the side panel.

Inside were wires, a blinking circuit board, and one tiny label:

GV MASTER NODE.

Barnaby spat the scrambler into my hand.

"Do it," he said.

I plugged it in.

The transmitter made a horrible buzzing noise.

Simba shrieked, "No!"

The red light turned green.

Then blue.

Then all the lights went out.

**[Doodle: Simba on top of the transmitter like a tiny general while Bea opens the side panel. Caption: "THE TRANSMITTER"]**

# Chapter 19: The Debate

For one second, the ridge was silent.

Then every cat in Riverstone started yowling at once.

Simba sprang onto the red box, fur puffed out until he looked twice his size.

"You fools!" he cried. "You have stopped the signal!"

Barnaby blinked. "That was the idea."

"You have stopped liberation!"

"No," I said. "You stopped that already."

Simba stared at me.

My hands shook. The transmitter buzzed under my fingers. Below us, cats and dogs and one very determined horse were tangled in the biggest animal argument Riverstone had ever seen.

But I was also angry.

Not hot angry. Not shouty angry.

Detective angry.

"You said animals deserve freedom," I said. "But you made them steal for you. You trapped Marmalade. You framed Barnaby. You used Mrs. Patel's fear. That isn't freedom. That's a cult with whiskers."

Simba's tail lashed. "Humans fear what they cannot control."

"So do you."

His eyes narrowed.

"You don't want animals to be free," I said. "You want them to obey you because you like being important."

Barnaby added, "Also, your salami is substandard."

Simba turned on him. "Do not insult the provisions."

"I insult them on principle."

Below us, Duke pinned two cats to the ground without scratching them. Henry backed the others toward the path. Feathered Frank circled overhead, shouting, "Simba is a fraud! Simba is a fraud! Simba has terrible tail balance!"

The cats hesitated.

Simba saw it.

His voice sharpened. "They follow me because I give them purpose."

"No," I said. "They followed you because they were hungry and you gave them speeches."

Barnaby nodded. "A dangerous combination."

I held up the scrambler. The transmitter light flickered.

"Maya's jammer is working," I said. "The police are coming. The food raids are failing."

Simba's ears flattened.

For the first time, he looked small.

Not nice.

Just small.

"You cannot understand," he hissed. "You have a home."

I looked at Barnaby.

He looked back.

"Yes," I said. "I do."

**[Doodle: Bea and Simba arguing with speech bubbles, while Barnaby stands between them looking bored. Caption: "THE DEBATE"]**

# Chapter 20: The Signal

Simba pressed a paw to the transmitter.

Nothing happened.

He pressed it again, harder.

Still nothing.

Barnaby smiled. "Technical difficulties. The enemy of every villain."

Simba's ears flattened. "Do not mistake malfunction for mercy."

He slapped the box with both paws. The transmitter gave a tiny buzz, like a bee trapped in a biscuit tin.

Maya's scrambler blinked inside the side panel. The red light flickered blue, then green, then blue again.

"She's interfering," I said.

Barnaby's tail thumped. "Splendidly."

Below us, Riverstone blinked in pieces. Phone torches. Fairground bulbs. Police lights winding through Daddy's cone spiral. The festival band had stopped playing, which meant even the humans knew something had gone wrong.

A voice crackled from the transmitter.

"...animals... of... Riverstone..."

Simba leapt onto the box. "Finally."

The signal broke apart.

"...move... now... left... no, right... biscuits... liberation... sausage..."

A group of cats at the bottom stopped raiding the burger van. Another group started following a pigeon. Three cats argued with a hedgehog that had no idea it was part of a revolution.

Barnaby tilted his head. "Did he just command them toward sausage?"

I peered over the edge. "Maya's jammer is working."

"How much time?"

Maya's voice crackled through the tiny communicator clipped to Barnaby's collar. "Signal is down to thirty percent. Also, police are entering the festival."

Simba's fur puffed out. "I will fix it myself."

I moved to block him.

He hissed. "You cannot defeat me."

Barnaby stepped beside me. "We are not trying to defeat you. We are trying to keep you from monologuing near electrical equipment."

Simba lunged.

Barnaby lunged back.

The transmitter sparked.

The whole ridge went silent.

Then, from below, came a sound I had never heard before.

Not yowling.

Not barking.

Cheering.

**[Doodle: Animals walking in confused circles while a broken signal bursts from the transmitter. Caption: "THE SIGNAL"]**

# Chapter 21: The Loyalists

The cheering came from the festival path.

Henry the Horse led the charge.

He came up the ridge like a brown thundercloud with ears. Behind him ran the Canine Resistance: Biscuit, Pip, Another Pip, and Colonel Socks, whose one sock flapped in the wind like a battle flag.

Feathered Frank flew above them, shouting, "Simba is a fraud! Simba is a fraud! Simba has terrible tail balance!"

The cats at the bottom stopped.

Some lowered their heads. Some looked embarrassed. One cat dropped a sack of cheese triangles.

Barnaby watched from the transmitter platform. "The pigeons are effective."

Frank puffed up. "I told you!"

Simba spat, "Traitors!"

Henry stopped beside Duke.

Duke looked at the cats, then at Simba.

Simba's voice sharpened. "Duke. Hold them."

Duke did not move.

"Duke!"

The Doberman sat down.

It was the calmest rebellion I had ever seen.

Barnaby's ears pricked. "Oh, this is excellent."

Duke looked up at Simba. "I'm muscle, not a fanatic."

Simba's claws clicked against the transmitter box. "You swore loyalty."

"I swore I would guard the ridge. I am guarding it from you."

The cats behind Duke hesitated.

Feathered Frank swooped lower. "Duke has defected! Duke has defected! Everyone act surprised!"

The Canine Resistance barked. Henry stamped. The cats scattered.

Lulu appeared behind the dogs, waving a traffic cone above her head.

"Mummy said I must stay on the path!" she shouted. "So I am on a path!"

"That is not the same path!" I called.

"It is a path!"

Barnaby sighed. "The small human has a point."

Simba backed toward the transmitter. "You think this changes anything? I still control the signal."

Maya's voice crackled again. "Signal is down to fifteen percent. Also, someone is stealing jam doughnuts from the festival stall."

"That is probably Lulu," I said.

"No," Lulu shouted. "That is probably justice!"

For one second, even Simba looked confused.

Then he ran.

**[Doodle: A wave of animals climbing Sentinel Ridge: Henry leading, dogs behind, Frank overhead. Caption: "THE LOYALISTS"]**

# Chapter 22: The Duel

Simba's elite guard surrounded the platform.

There were six of them. Three were large. Two were fast. One wore a tiny black mask, which felt unnecessary.

Duke stepped onto the platform behind us.

Barnaby faced him.

For a moment, neither moved.

Then Barnaby said, "You could have joined us sooner."

Duke's jaw tightened. "I had to be sure."

"Of what?"

"That he would lose."

Barnaby snorted. "Romantic."

"Practical."

The masked cat sprang.

Barnaby ducked. Duke caught the cat gently but firmly by the scruff and set him aside like a parcel.

Another cat darted for me.

I held up my notebook.

The cat stopped, confused.

Barnaby barked. Duke stepped between us.

Together, they moved. Not like friends. Not exactly. Like two people who had argued for years and suddenly discovered they agreed on the important bit.

Barnaby blocked left. Duke blocked right. A spaniel named Pip bit a tail, then apologised to it. Henry sneezed again, which cleared half the platform.

Simba backed against the transmitter.

"Duke!" he shrieked. "You are betraying animal liberation!"

Duke pinned the last guard without scratching him. "You are betraying lunch."

Barnaby nodded. "Accurate."

The platform fell quiet.

Simba's eyes darted between us, Duke, the transmitter, and the path below where police lights flashed through the trees.

"You don't understand," he said. "If I fail, they will put me back on a lead."

Barnaby's voice softened. "Perhaps."

Simba flinched.

"But," Barnaby continued, "you built this mess because you wanted a throne. Not freedom. Not safety. A throne."

Simba's tail lashed. "I deserved one."

"No," Barnaby said. "You deserved a nap and stricter boundaries."

I plugged Maya's scrambler deeper into the box.

The transmitter gave one last buzz.

Then the signal died.

**[Doodle: Barnaby and Duke back-to-back facing cats on the transmitter platform. Caption: "THE DUEL"]**

# Chapter 23: The Salami Solution

Just when we thought it was over, Simba ran.

He shot across the platform, leapt onto a lower branch, and bounced toward the festival path.

"After him!" I shouted.

Everyone moved.

Unfortunately, everyone moved in different directions.

The dogs ran downhill. Frank flew uphill. Henry turned left because a rabbit crossed the path. Lulu ran straight toward the biggest noise, which is how Lulu navigates life.

Simba reached the old storage shed below the ridge.

Inside was the 12kg Artisan Salami.

Book 1 salami.

The legendary salami.

The salami Barnaby had once described as "the only cured meat with a future".

Simba dragged it into the open with both paws.

Barnaby stopped dead. "No."

Simba grinned. "You fight with clues. I fight with meat."

He rolled the salami down the slope.

Every cat in Riverstone turned.

The food-raiding cats. The Meow-fia guards. The confused festival cats. Even the hedgehog looked interested, though I don't think hedgehogs eat salami.

The salami rolled.

The cats followed.

It was like watching a very greedy comet.

Barnaby stared after it. "That is a waste of excellent pork."

"Focus," I said.

"I am focusing on the tragedy."

Simba used the distraction to leap toward the road. Police lights flashed below. Mummy's voice cut through the trees.

"Beatrice Chen! Stay exactly where you are!"

This was not helpful because I was already exactly where I was.

Duke sprinted after Simba. Barnaby ran beside him. I ran after Barnaby, which is how most dangerous things happen in my life.

The salami rolled into the festival crowd.

Cats piled after it.

Someone shouted, "Is this part of the show?"

Daddy shouted, "I think the salami is in charge!"

Mummy did not shout. That was worse.

She had the clipboard.

**[Doodle: Cats swarming a giant salami while Bea runs past with Barnaby. Caption: "THE SALAMI SOLUTION"]**

# Chapter 24: The Arrest

Mummy's clipboard defeated Simba.

I do not know how else to explain it.

She stood at the edge of the festival road with police officers behind her, Daddy beside her holding a traffic cone, and Lulu behind her holding half a jam sandwich.

"David," Mummy said, "the cone."

Daddy lifted the cone.

Simba, running full speed toward the road, saw it.

He tried to stop.

He tried to turn.

He tried to look dignified while sliding across wet grass.

He failed at all three.

Duke landed beside him.

Barnaby landed beside Duke.

I landed beside Barnaby, mostly because my foot slipped.

Simba was caught under Daddy's cone.

For three seconds, Riverstone cheered.

Then Feathered Frank shouted, "PRISONER SECURED! PRISONER SECURED! SOMEONE FIND HIM A SMALLER HAT!"

A police officer picked up the cone.

Simba was gone.

Only a ginger hair and a tiny black ribbon remained.

Mummy stared at the empty grass. "He escaped?"

Barnaby sniffed the ribbon. "Cowardice with accessories."

Duke lowered his head. "He had a second route."

"Of course he did," I said.

Maya arrived, breathless, with her detector smoking slightly. "Signal is dead. Food raids stopped. Mrs. Patel's sister's cat is safe. Also, my prototype may need a new battery."

Mummy pulled me into a hug so tight my ribs squeaked.

"You are in so much trouble," she whispered.

"Is it big trouble or normal trouble?"

"New-category trouble."

Daddy hugged us both. "I am very proud and also confused."

Barnaby sat beside me. "For the record, Beatrice solved the case."

Mummy looked at him.

Then she looked at me.

Then she sighed. "Yes. She did."

Barnaby's tail thumped once.

*'Naturally.'*

**[Doodle: Simba trapped under a traffic cone in front of police lights, looking furious. Caption: "THE ARREST"]**

# Chapter 25: The New Normal

Riverstone returned to normal on Monday.

Normal meant:
- The bakery had sausage rolls again.
- The school kitchen had cheese triangles again.
- Mr. Pritchard stopped guarding his chocolate mice with a cricket bat.
- Mrs. Patel reopened the Crumb & Kettle and gave Barnaby a plain biscuit "for bravery".
- Barnaby complained for twelve minutes but ate it.

Marmalade came home with Mrs. Patel's sister. She wore a tiny blue ribbon and looked pleased with herself.

Duke visited the Crumb & Kettle every afternoon. He sat by the door and pretended not to wait for sausage scraps. Mrs. Patel pretended not to give him any.

Feathered Frank started a newsletter. It was mostly complaints.

Henry the Horse became famous for sneezing on a criminal. He accepted this with dignity and extra apples.

The police found the Glass Vaults relay nodes and questioned the humans who had helped Simba. Mummy said I was not allowed to help with that part because I was "too involved". This was unfair because being involved is basically my whole brand.

At the Crumb & Kettle, everyone celebrated.

Maya brought her broken detector and called it "evidence". Lulu brought crayons and drew a picture of herself defeating the salami. Mummy brought a clipboard and a list of rules beginning with "No more secret ridge climbs".

Barnaby received a new rug from Mrs. Patel.

It was blue.

It was soft.

It was exactly the right size for pretending not to enjoy attention.

Barnaby sat on it in the middle of the café while everyone thanked him.

"I accept your gratitude," he said. "And your biscuits."

I sat beside him with my notebook.

The front page had a new title:

THE SENTINEL RIDGE SHOWDOWN: CASE CLOSED

Underneath, I wrote one more line.

My dog is still judging me. But now, the whole town knows he's right.

**[Doodle: Bea, Barnaby, Maya, Lulu, Henry, and Frank at a café table with the caption: "THE END... FOR NOW"]**
