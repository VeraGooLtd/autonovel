# Chapter 14: The Regroup

The base of Sentinel Ridge was muddy, cold, and full of animals pretending not to be frightened.

The spaniels huddled under Henry's belly. Feathered Frank kept saying, "I am calm," which usually means the opposite. Maya's detector sat on a flat stone, blinking like it knew something we didn't.

Barnaby shook the last of the sand from his fur.

"Do not stand there," I said.

He moved two centimetres.

"Barnaby."

He moved another centimetre.

Maya crouched beside the detector. "The main signal is still active. Stronger now."

"Because Simba knows we're coming," I said.

Barnaby sat beside me. "Or because he is vain enough to increase the volume."

Lulu appeared with mud on her nose. "I found a stick."

"It is a branch," Maya said.

"It is a big stick."

Barnaby nodded. "Terminology is not the mission."

I opened my notebook. The pages were damp and wrinkled. My evidence map looked like a spider had learned geography.

"Okay," I said. "We know Simba's plan. Blackout at midnight. Food raids across Riverstone. Human helpers opening storage. Transmitter on Sentinel Ridge."

Maya pointed at the detector. "If I jam from the generator hut below, the signal will stutter. Not stop. Stutter."

"How long?"

"Maybe ten minutes. Maybe less."

Barnaby looked up the ridge. "Ten minutes is enough to reach the transmitter if we are quick."

"And if we're not?"

"Then we shall be very slow and very caught."

I stared at my notes. There were gaps. Too many gaps. Human helpers. Exact route. Number of cats. Whether Mummy could get police through the festival crowd. Whether Mrs. Patel was safe. Whether Marmalade had been moved.

My brain started making lists too fast.

Human helpers: unknown.
Police route: unknown.
Cats: too many.
Marmalade: unknown.
Transmitter: unknown.
Barnaby: probably about to do something brave and stupid.

Barnaby nudged my notebook down.

"Hey."

"Your lists are eating you."

"They are not."

"They are. I can hear them chewing."

Maya looked at me. "Bea, you don't have to know everything."

"I kind of do. That's how plans work."

Barnaby's expression softened, which on a bulldog looks like someone has turned down the grumpiness by half a notch.

"Beatrice," he said, "you have mapped the crime, questioned witnesses, survived a kidnapping, and recruited a horse. These are not small achievements."

"Mostly Lulu recruited the horse."

Henry nodded. "I recruited myself."

Barnaby continued, "You have also been right when the adults were wrong."

"That sounds like something I should write down."

"It is. Write this too." He lifted his chin. "You are a capable collaborator, Bea."

The ridge went quiet.

Even Frank stopped fluttering.

I looked at Barnaby.

Barnaby looked away as if he had accidentally dropped a biscuit.

"Thank you," I said.

"Do not make it sentimental."

"I won't."

Maya smiled. "Too late."

I closed my notebook.

For once, I didn't need another list. I knew what to do.

Trust the plan. Trust the team. Trust the weird dog voice in my head.
