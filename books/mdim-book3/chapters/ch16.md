# Chapter 16: The Festival

Riverstone Festival began with balloons, brass bands, and the smell of hot dough.

It is hard to stop a criminal mastermind when everyone else is trying to win a giant teddy bear.

Mummy had a clipboard the size of a door.

"Bea, stay where I can see you."

"I will."

"That was too quick."

"I will try to stay where you can see me."

"Better."

Daddy was wearing his high-visibility jacket, even though he was not officially helping. He had been assigned to move cones for the parade route. This was dangerous because Daddy sees a cone and immediately believes it is part of a bigger project.

"David," Mummy said, "do not improve the road layout."

"I'm just making it clearer."

"You are making it impossible."

He had placed cones in a spiral.

A spiral.

Somehow, this helped us.

At five past seven, the first police car tried to turn onto the festival road and got stuck behind Daddy's cone maze. At ten past, a bakery van took one look and reversed into the school car park. At quarter past, three cats carrying a sack of flour had to abandon it because Henry the Horse stepped into their path and sneezed.

Feathered Frank landed on my shoulder.

"Diversion working!"

"Good," I whispered.

"Also the horse sneezed on a cat."

"Also good."

Maya was already gone, heading toward the generator hut with her detector bag. The dogs had vanished into the festival crowd. Lulu was supposed to stay with Mummy, which meant she would either be safe or become mayor by midnight.

Barnaby and I slipped behind the community hall.

The festival lights glittered above us. Children laughed. A brass band played something cheerful and slightly wrong. Nobody knew that across town, cats were waiting in cupboards and storerooms.

I did.

That is the problem with knowing secrets. They make ordinary things feel like they are holding their breath.

Barnaby walked beside me. "You are doing the quiet face."

"What quiet face?"

"The one where your mouth goes flat and your brain makes alarm noises."

"I'm fine."

"Liar."

I smiled despite myself. "You can hear my brain?"

"I can hear your brain. I can hear three spaniels arguing about a sausage. I can hear Frank lying about being brave."

From above, Frank shouted, "I heard that!"

We reached the lower ridge path.

The festival sounds faded behind us.

Ahead, Sentinel Ridge rose black against the sky.

At its top, the transmitter blinked.

47... 46... 45...

Maya's countdown had started.
