# Chapter 10: The Signal

We ran.

Not in a dignified detective way. In a "there are probably cats behind us" way.

Henry carried Lulu because she said running made her knees noisy. Feathered Frank flew ahead, shouting updates nobody could understand. Maya kept looking at the detector as if it might turn into a better detector if she glared hard enough.

Barnaby ran beside me, breathing hard.

"You all right?" I asked.

"I am furious. It uses a great deal of energy."

At the park, Maya stopped under the climbing frame and plugged the broken relay into her laptop.

"This serial number is from my parents' company," she said. "Festival temporary nodes. They were meant to power security cameras and crowd counters."

"So Simba stole them?"

"Or someone from Glass Vaults gave him access."

Mummy had said the police would help. But police need proof, and proof is difficult when half of it is a dog talking in your head.

Maya clicked a key. A map appeared on the screen. Red dots blinked across Riverstone.

"Every node is active," she said.

Barnaby peered at the laptop. "Can it be stopped?"

"From here? No. The master signal is coming from Sentinel Ridge. If I can get close enough, I might jam it."

"How close?"

Maya swallowed. "Close enough to be shouted at by cats."

"Risky."

"Yes."

"I like it."

The laptop made a sharp sound.

Maya's face changed. "Transmission incoming."

Static crackled from the speakers.

Then a voice filled the park.

"Citizens of Riverstone."

Barnaby's lips curled. "Oh, here we go."

"I am Simba. The Ginger Ninja. The true voice of the animals."

A few pigeons on the swings cooed.

"Not now," Frank hissed from a branch.

Simba continued. "For too long, humans have controlled our meals, our beds, our doors, and our dignity. At midnight during the Riverstone Festival, the lights will fall. The cupboards will open. The age of kibble schedules ends."

Barnaby muttered, "Says the cat who expects servants to open tins."

"Tonight, animals will take what is theirs. Tonight, Riverstone will learn who truly rules."

The transmission cut out.

Maya stared at the countdown on her detector.

47:12:04

"We have less than forty-eight hours," she said.

Barnaby looked up at Sentinel Ridge. "Then we need Barnaby captured."

I stared at him. "What?"

"It is the obvious next move."

"It is not obvious. It is horrible."

"If Simba wants a trial, I shall give him one. From inside."

"No."

"Bea—"

"No. You are not getting yourself captured because you want to be dramatic."

Barnaby sat back. "I am not being dramatic. I am being strategic."

"You enjoy both."

He looked offended. "Accurate, but hurtful."

Maya closed the laptop. "Barnaby's right about one thing. If he gets inside Simba's camp, we learn where Marmalade is held."

"And where the transmitter is."

Barnaby nodded. "Precisely."

I hated that. I hated that he was right. I hated that being a detective meant sometimes letting your best friend walk into a trap.

Barnaby touched his nose to my hand.

"I will be careful," he said.

In my head, quieter, he added, "And if I am not, you will be brilliant enough to fix it."
