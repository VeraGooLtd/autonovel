# Chapter 9: The Boathouse

We went back down the ridge because Maya said the broken relay had a serial number and she needed to compare it with her parents' records.

This was how we ended up at Simba's old Boathouse HQ.

The boathouse sat beside the canal, half-hidden by reeds. In Book 1, Simba had used it as a secret base. In real life, it smelled like damp wood, old rope, and cat arrogance.

Barnaby stopped at the door.

"I know that smell," he said.

"You can't smell arrogance."

"I can when it has been left in a room for too long."

Inside, the boathouse was dark. Maya switched on her torch. The beam moved over crates, old life jackets, and a wall covered in paper.

Maps.

Lots of maps.

Riverstone maps. School maps. Festival route maps. Storage room layouts. Cupboard labels. Delivery times.

My notebook suddenly felt very small.

Maya walked to the wall. "He's mapped every food store in town."

Barnaby sniffed a crate. "Tinned tuna. Dried biscuits. Emergency chocolate. He has been planning this for months."

Lulu picked up a paper crown. "This one is sparkly."

"Don't touch—"

Too late. A string snapped.

A curtain dropped from the ceiling.

Behind it stood Duke.

He was bigger than I remembered. A Doberman with a black coat, amber eyes, and the sort of face that made you check whether you had done something wrong before you even knew what it was.

Barnaby stepped in front of me.

"Duke."

Duke looked at him. "Barnaby."

They stared at each other.

It was not a friendly stare.

It was not exactly an unfriendly stare either. It was more like two people recognising an old argument.

"You're working for him," Barnaby said.

Duke's ears flicked. "I am working near him."

"That is a fancy sentence for yes."

Duke looked at me. "The girl should go home."

"I'm not going home while Simba is stealing food."

"He is not stealing. He is liberating."

Barnaby barked once. It echoed off the canal walls. "He is stealing with adjectives."

Duke's mouth twitched. Almost a smile. Almost.

Maya held up the relay label. "Duke, did you know these came from Glass Vaults?"

Duke looked away.

That was answer enough.

"Your humans installed them?" I asked.

"Some humans think animals should be free from rules," Duke said. "They like Simba's speeches."

"Animals are free," I said. "They just also need dinner."

Duke looked at Barnaby. "You taught her that?"

"No," Barnaby said. "She learned it despite me."

A noise came from outside. Paws. Many paws.

Duke's head turned. "You should leave. Now."

"Why?"

"Because if Simba finds you here, he will not send cats next time."

Barnaby's fur lifted. "What does that mean?"

Duke stepped aside from the door. "It means the trial begins at midnight."

"Trial?"

Duke's eyes moved to Barnaby.

"Yours."
